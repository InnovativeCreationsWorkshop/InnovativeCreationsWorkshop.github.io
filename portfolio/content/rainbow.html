RainbowTrace

A Body in Motion

Technology Used: TouchDesigner, Microsoft Kinect

This project aimed to create an engaging and interactive experience using the Microsoft Kinect, focusing on hand movement tracking rather than particle-based effects. The goal was to offer users a visually stimulating and fun way to create dynamic art, with color-driven interactions and audio-reactive elements. By leveraging the Kinect's hand tracking capabilities, users could manipulate a virtual canvas, and the visual output would respond to the movement of their hands as well as the audio input.

To begin, I eliminated the particle effects in favor of hand-tracking data from the Microsoft Kinect. This allowed me to map the user's hand movements to control tracing abilities on the screen. The light-based components were removed, and instead, I provided a color palette designed to be visually enticing and fun to interact with, achieved through the use of a blend mode. The Kinect's player index feature was essential for detecting the user's position and starting the hand-tracking process.

The experience was further enhanced by incorporating audio-reactive elements. A generative visualizer was embedded within the user's silhouette, changing shape and morphing in response to the audio input. This feature created a dynamic, responsive interaction between the user's movements and the sound environment.

User Experience

The tracking mechanism itself was similar to the interactive light piece, but instead of following light, it traced the movements of the user's hands. I used a series of mathematical nodes to accurately track the movement of the hands, which in turn generated points to draw lines on the screen.

In terms of usability, this experience was designed to be Covid-friendly, limiting interaction to a single user at a time. However, I observed that groups of users were often comfortable participating together, leading me to consider the possibility of expanding the experience to accommodate multiple users simultaneously.

Observations:

I found the behavior of users to be intriguing. Most individuals instinctively adopted symmetrical movements, with their arms often mirroring each other to create balanced shapes on the screen. Additionally, I observed users using their entire body, not just their hands, to influence the visual output, which added an interesting layer of interaction.

This project successfully merged technology, art, and audio-reactivity into a single, interactive experience. The use of hand-tracking with the Microsoft Kinect allowed for an intuitive and immersive way for users to create and interact with dynamic visuals. Through further refinement, such as considering a two-user experience, this concept could be expanded to increase interactivity and engagement.
